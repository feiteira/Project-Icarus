== Maritime Piracy and the Collapse of the Rules-Based Order ==

Even during the ceasefire period, American forces escalated by boarding and seizing an Iranian cargo ship returning from China. The vessel was suspected of carrying fuel and ballistic missile components. When the crew refused to stop, American forces fired on the engine and boarded with special forces. Professor Jiang describes this as an act of piracy under international law -- the seizure of a lawful commercial vessel in international waters without a United Nations mandate. Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth had previously argued that nations must allow free navigation of the seas while simultaneously asserting that America retains the right to seize ships at will. This double standard, Professor Jiang argues, reveals the rules-based international order as a facade: the rules apply to everyone except America. Within days of this incident, two additional oil tankers were seized by American forces, extending the maritime blockade.

== The War's True Strategic Purpose ==

The Israel-Iran war is not primarily about Israel's security, nuclear weapons, or any of the stated justifications. Professor Jiang identifies its purpose as the closure of the North-South trade corridor through Iran, which links Russia to Asian markets via the Persian Gulf. Simultaneously, the destruction of Middle Eastern energy infrastructure through refinery sabotage and military strikes removes the primary alternative supply source for China, which depends on Middle Eastern oil for approximately 60 percent of its needs. With Iran at war and the Strait of Malacca controllable through the first island chain, China has no viable path to the energy resources it requires. The result is a world forced to purchase American oil, American weapons, and American financing -- denominated in dollars. This analysis situates the conflict within the broader framework of [[Great Power Competition]] and the [[Imperial Cycle]] that characterizes American hegemonic strategy. The systematic targeting of energy infrastructure across the region, including the [[Oil Refinery Attacks|refinery strikes in Kuwait and the Emirates]], plays a central role in this strategic calculus.

== America's Domestic Political Consensus ==

The war enjoys bipartisan support that defies normal political polarization. When Congress voted on restricting the president's war powers, the outcome was pre-arranged theater: enough Republicans and Democrats cooperated secretly to ensure the resolution failed regardless of how individual members voted. Professor Jiang interprets this as evidence that both establishment parties support the imperial project. Democrats have abandoned any anti-war platform, recognizing that opposing the war would benefit neither their electoral prospects nor their institutional interests. The national security apparatus -- the permanent government of generals and intelligence officials -- drives policy regardless of which party holds the presidency.

== What Round Two Looks Like ==

If the ceasefire collapses, Professor Jiang expects a significant escalation in Round Two. American forces would target Iranian civilian infrastructure -- bridges, universities, reservoirs, power plants, desalination facilities -- in an attempt to collapse Iran's capacity to function as a nation-state. Iran would respond by expanding its counterattacks beyond the Strait of Hormuz to include the Red Sea and major gas pipelines, potentially taking one-third of the world's energy supply offline. The economic damage would be severe, potentially triggering a global depression. The National Defense Strategy explicitly accepts this outcome as an acceptable cost of maintaining American hegemony.
