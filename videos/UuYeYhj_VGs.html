People often misunderstand what drives Donald  Trump. The common assumption is that his decisions are rooted in ideology, in a conservative vision  for America, or in partisan battles against the political left. But if you study Trump carefully,  if you trace his behavior across four decades of public life, a different and more consistent  pattern emerges. Trump is fundamentally obsessed with power. Not ordinary political power measured  in poll numbers and legislative wins, but the kind of sweeping transformational power that places a  person's name permanently in the history books. Once you understand that obsession, once you  accept it as the central organizing principle of everything he does, his fixation on Iran stops  looking like a foreign policy position and starts looking like something far more personal. To  understand Trump's relationship with Iran, you first have to understand how Trump sees  himself in the context of history. This is not a recent development. Go back to the 1980s  and you will find Trump giving interviews about American weakness, about bad deals, about  leaders who lack the toughness to stand up for their country's interests. Even then, decades  before he ever considered running for office, Trump was positioning himself mentally as the  kind of figure who could step in, take charge, and reverse the decline. That self-image never  changed. It only grew more elaborate and more ambitious as the years passed. Trump does not  drink, does not smoke, and avoids the kinds of indulgences that people often associate with  his flashy public persona. The gold towers and the beauty pageantss and the celebrity lifestyle  create the impression of a reckless personality. But the reality is more disciplined than that.  Sustained ambition of the kind Trump has displayed across 40 years requires a certain kind of  focused self-control. You do not spend four decades carefully constructing a public image  without discipline. And that is precisely what Trump was doing long before he entered politics.  The Apprentice was not merely entertainment. It was a yearslong exercise in political branding,  introducing millions of Americans to the idea that Trump was the decisive leader who could walk  into a failing organization and immediately impose order. That image embedded itself in the  national imagination and did not fade. When Trump finally entered politics, he was not starting from  scratch. He was stepping into a role he had spent decades rehearsing. And once in office, it became  clear that he did not think the way traditional politicians think. Most politicians measure  success in four-year cycles. They worry about midterm elections and approval ratings and the  daily news cycle. Trump thinks in terms of legacy. He wants to occupy a place in history alongside  leaders who fundamentally reshaped the world. That ambition requires not just victories but  historic victories. And that is where Iran enters the picture. Iran is not a simple adversary.  It is a civilization with thousands of years of continuous history. A regional power with a  sophisticated network of alliances stretching from Lebanon through Iraq and into Yemen. A state that  has survived invasion, international isolation, assassinations of its key figures, and decades  of relentless economic pressure. Iran has built its national identity in substantial part around  the act of resisting American dominance. For a leader looking for an opponent whose defeat  would prove transformational greatness, Iran is almost perfectly suited to the role.  Defeating Iran or even forcing a fundamental shift in Iranian behavior would represent an achievement  that generations of American presidents attempted and failed to accomplish. Trump understands that  arithmetic. His worldview treats international relations as a series of negotiations between  powerful actors where there are winners and losers, strong leaders and weak ones, and  where the objective is always to dominate the terms of engagement. From that perspective,  every American president who tried to manage the relationship with Iran through compromise  and multilateral agreements was a weak leader who accepted unsatisfactory outcomes. Trump's  answer to that was the maximum pressure campaign, a strategy built on the belief that intensifying  economic and diplomatic pressure until the system cracks is more effective than seeking managed  accommodations. Sanctions tightened. Economic conditions inside Iran deteriorated. Regional  tensions climbed. The logic was deliberate and consistent. An adversary under enough pressure  will eventually have no choice but to accept new terms. Critics of this approach argue that it  was reckless, that it brought the region closer to open conflict without producing meaningful change  in Iranian behavior, and that ordinary Iranians bore the heaviest costs.
