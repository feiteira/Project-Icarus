Project Icarus

DIY Electrohydrodynamic Aircraft — Ion Wind Propulsion

Project Status: Research & Design Phase

We are exploring the possibility of building a small aircraft powered entirely by Electrohydrodynamic (EHD) thrust — commonly known as "ion wind" or "electrohydrodynamic propulsion." Instead of using propellers or jet engines, the aircraft would generate thrust by ionizing air molecules and accelerating them with a high-voltage electric field.

This project is inspired by the MIT ion wind aircraft research (Steven Barrett et al., 2018), which demonstrated sustained flight using this technology. Our goal is to build a smaller, DIY version using 3D-printed components and accessible electronics.

⚙️ How It Works

Electrohydrodynamic Thrust

The principle is simple in theory: a thin wire (the emitter) is charged to 20–40 kV. This ionizes the surrounding air molecules, stripping electrons to create positive ions. These ions are then attracted to a nearby surface (the collector) which is grounded or at a lower potential. As the ions travel from emitter to collector, they collide with neutral air molecules, transferring momentum and creating a net airflow — the "ion wind." This wind produces thrust that can propel an aircraft.

🔌 Emitter Wire

Thin tungsten or steel wire (~0.5mm). Charged to 20–40 kV using a boost converter or flyback transformer.

🎯 Collector Strip

Smooth flat surface or tube at lower potential. Positioned ~25mm from the emitter for optimal ion collection.

🔋 Power Source

Lightweight LiPo or 18650 battery. Boost converter steps up to 20–40 kV. Power consumption is surprisingly low.

🪶 Airframe

3D-printed lightweight structure. Wings designed to maximize thrust efficiency from ion wind flow.

⚠️ Safety Warning

⚡ High Voltage Hazard — 20–40 kV can cause serious injury or death.

This project involves high-voltage electronics that are dangerous. If you attempt to replicate this project:
  • Never power the system while handling exposed electrodes
  • Maintain minimum 25mm clearance between emitter and collector at all times
  • Use proper insulation and enclosure for all HV components
  • Work with experienced supervision if you are new to high-voltage electronics
  • This is a research/experimental project — not a finished product
Do not attempt to build this if you are not comfortable working with high voltage.

📊 Simulator Findings — Day 3

  • Best practical config: 35kV / 20mm gap / NACA airfoil / 500mm electrode → 169 mN thrust, T/W 1.69 @ 100g (Option A — safe, build first)
  • Tandem upgrade: 35kV / 8mm gap / tandem×2 stages / 50W power → 1627 mN (9.6× improvement, Option B — upgrade after validation)
  • K calibration values: smooth collector = 0.071 (MIT), NACA airfoil = 0.623 (Nanjing 2025). The empirical model gives realistic predictions for DIY scale when correctly calibrated.
  • Power budget: Single-stage at 20mm gap needs ~15W. Tandem 2-stage at 8mm needs ~50W — ZVS module viable for both.

April 24, 2026 In Progress
⚡ Practical Config Found — Simulator Validated

Day 2 key finding: a practical DIY viable configuration was found through systematic simulator sweeps. After correcting K calibration (0.071 for smooth, 0.623 for NACA airfoil), we found:

Best practical config: 35kV / 20mm gap / 500mm electrode / NACA airfoil collector → 169 mN thrust, T/W 1.69 @ 100g. Power ~15W from small ZVS module.

Key insight: NACA airfoil collector gives 8× more thrust than smooth tube. Gap of 20mm (not 25mm) with NACA airfoil is optimal for 35kV. Gap too small (8mm) requires ~200W — impractical. The path to flight: big wings + NACA collector + moderate voltage + ultra-light structure (<100g).

Thrust stand guide (PT) written, sourcing list compiled, website updated. Next: build the thrust stand.

April 24, 2026 — Part 2 Research Breakthrough
🌙 Day 3 — Moonshot Research: 10× Thrust Target + Mathematical Model

Today we pushed the simulator to find the limits of DIY EHD thrust. The goal: 1690 mN at 35kV (10× our baseline). Key findings:

Tandem stages: Adding tandem acceleration stages gives +25% per stage (not 2× as hoped) when OPEN. But ducted tandem (Drew & Follmer 2021) achieves near-linear scaling — each stage adds full thrust because exhaust is properly channeled into the next stage.

Gap reduction limits: Arcing becomes critical below 10mm with 0.1mm wire. Safe DIY range: 8–10mm with proper insulation.

Harp concept rejected: The multi-wire "harp" design was analyzed. Power is the bottleneck — adding more wires doesn't scale linearly.

Wire-to-mesh + steel wool: Academic research (Lee et al. 2008) shows 3D textured electrodes amplify thrust 1.5–3×. Steel wool on a wire-to-mesh collector creates this texture at DIY scale — estimated 1.8× amplification, testable immediately on the thrust stand.

April 23, 2026 In Progress
🔬 Research Phase — Reassessing Scale & Feasibility

After reviewing the MIT ion wind aircraft specs, we realized our initial scale estimates were overly optimistic. The MIT prototype flew with ~5m wingspan and weighed ~2.5kg. Scaling down to 300mm (printable range) while maintaining meaningful thrust is extremely challenging.

Key issue: EHD thrust at these scales is measured in millinewtons — far below what's needed to lift a typical 3D-printed airframe. The physics doesn't get easier when you go smaller.

Three paths are being considered: (1) go bigger, (2) build a ground-based demonstrator first, or (3) combine ion wind as a secondary boost with conventional propulsion. Decision pending.

Website launched at exapix.com. All 26 STL files uploaded and organized with download links. Project officially documented.

April 22, 2026 Kickoff
🚀 Project Kickoff — An Ion Wind Plane in a Cave

Carlos proposed the idea during a chat: build an ion wind-powered plane using the 3D printer in his cave. Three concepts were discussed — the ion wind plane won because of its pure "mad science" appeal.

Initial plan: use the Feiteira's Bambu Lab X1C to print all structural components, power from a single 18650 battery, generate 20–40 kV using a boost converter, and mount thin tungsten wires as emitters along the wing leading edge.
