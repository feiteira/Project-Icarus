== Professor Jiang's Analysis of the Middle East ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of the Middle East draws from eight videos across his predictive history, Civilization, and strategy series. His central argument is that the current conflict cannot be understood through the conventional frameworks of geopolitics, nationalism, or resource competition alone, but must be situated within an eschatological framework that motivates certain actors to pursue outcomes that rational cost-benefit analysis would forbid. The Middle East is not simply a region undergoing conflict; it is the crucible in which the global balance of power for the next several decades is being determined, and the outcome will be shaped by structural forces that are already in motion and cannot be reversed by any single intervention.

The United States has been implementing a consistent strategy of regime change in the Middle East for twenty years, a strategy Professor Jiang describes as having three pillars. The first is decapitation of leadership, removing the entire command structure of the target state. The second is economic sabotage through sanctions that destroy the civilian infrastructure, including water and electricity, that allows a population to function as a coherent society. The third is fermenting sectarian violence through divide-and-conquer tactics that activate ethnic and religious conflicts within the target population. Professor Jiang applies this framework to Iraq, where the de-Ba'athification policy removed the entire elite from government, military, and police, and the result was not democracy but a prolonged sectarian conflict that continues to this day. He argues that anyone who does not understand the consequences of this policy in Iraq either is not paying attention or has a stake in not understanding.

The Iranian conflict represents the culminating test of whether this American strategy can succeed against a state with the depth, motivation, and external support to resist it. Professor Jiang's analysis identifies a fundamental problem with the American approach: Iran has the geography, the population, and the external alliances to absorb decapitation strikes and transform them into strategic advantages. When American strikes kill Iranian leaders, they solve the overproduction problem of too many ambitious leaders competing for influence, producing a leaner and more effective command structure. When American bombing destroys Iranian cities, it unites the previously divided urban secular population with the rural religious population, creating a more cohesive and motivated society. When American money arms Iranian minorities, it activates Persian nationalism, which the theocracy had suppressed in favor of a transethnic religious identity. Every element of the American strategy produces the opposite of its intended effect.

The Iranian strategy, as Professor Jiang analyzes it, centers on the destruction of the global economy through attacks on the energy infrastructure of the GCC states. Qatar provides twenty percent of the world's liquefied natural gas. The Strait of Hormuz carries the oil that powers the global economy. Iran cannot win a direct military confrontation with the United States, but it can make the economic consequences of the conflict so severe that they exceed what the global system can absorb. This is Professor Jiang's key insight: the Iranian strategy is not military but economic, and the military actions are in service of an economic collapse that would force the United States to withdraw not from victory but from exhaustion. China's structural dependency on Middle Eastern energy means that Beijing has no choice but to support Iran covertly, providing weapons and financing while maintaining the formal posture of neutrality. Russia shares this interest, which creates the conditions for a de facto alliance among Iran, China, and Russia against American regional hegemony.

The three flashpoints Professor Jiang identifies as most likely to escalate the conflict beyond its current scope are the ground invasion question, the nuclear question, and the Al-Aqsa mosque. A ground invasion would trap the United States in Iran for five to ten years, requiring a national draft and producing a casualty level that would destroy American political will for the entire enterprise. Nuclear weapons will not be used, Professor Jiang predicts with high confidence, because the strategic and political costs of breaking that taboo would exceed any tactical benefit. But the Al-Aqsa mosque will almost certainly be destroyed, either by Israeli extremists seeking to rebuild the Third Temple or by the collateral damage of sustained regional warfare, and that destruction would obligate the world's two billion Muslims to respond with whatever means are available. Each of these flashpoints is individually more likely than not, and together they form a trajectory toward catastrophic escalation that the structural logic of the conflict makes very difficult to avoid.
