== Professor Jiang's Analysis of War ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of War draws from seven videos in Professor Jiang's Predictive History corpus. His framework centers on a fundamental structural insight: modern American military power rests on an inverted cost pyramid that makes prolonged ground war functionally impossible to win. The pyramid inverts because air power, naval assets, and advanced technology are the most expensive elements of the US military while infantry soldiers, the cheapest unit, form the smallest component. This means that in any sustained conflict against a peer or near-peer adversary, the United States cannot replenish its most critical assets fast enough to maintain pressure. Precision munitions consumed in weeks take months to replace. Interceptor missiles cost millions while Iranian drones cost thousands. This mathematics of attrition, Professor Jiang argues, is more decisive than any individual battlefield outcome.

The historical precedent for this dynamic is the 1991 Gulf War, which Professor Jiang describes as a video game rather than a genuine conflict. The overwhelming US technological advantage against a devastated Iraqi adversary created a dangerous illusion that air power alone could settle geopolitical disputes. That illusion, he contends, has never been tested against a real opponent. When such a test comes with Iran, the first failed air campaign will create a political crisis that forces the United States toward an ever-wider commitment or an ever-more-humiliating withdrawal. Neither outcome resembles what the American public was sold when the operation began.

What makes the Iran war uniquely dangerous is that leaving it is not a clean option. Professor Jiang draws a distinction between starting a war and escaping a war as two entirely separate skills. Once a conflict begins reshaping regional hierarchies, alliance credibility, domestic politics, financial expectations, and energy infrastructure, withdrawal does not simply stop the bleeding. It opens deeper wounds. The war has become structural. The power vacuum, the economic transfers, the credibility of alliance commitments, the political survival of leaders who sold the war at home, all of these systems are now in motion. To stop is to trigger cascades in every direction simultaneously. The ugliest wars in history, Professor Jiang notes, are rarely the ones leaders planned to fight forever. They are the ones they discovered they could no longer end.

Equally critical is the legitimacy paradox that any American-installed Iranian government would face. Professor Jiang draws on Vietnam, Afghanistan, and Iraq as historical examples demonstrating that foreign-imposed leaders are perceived as traitors by their own populations precisely because foreign powers chose them. This destroys the leader's domestic credibility before they take office, rendering them unable to unite factions, rally populations, or survive the withdrawal of American support. The lesson is structural, not contingent: it applies to every country at every time, because the logic of legitimacy does not vary with culture or geography. Trump's attempt to select Iran's next supreme leader replicates every mistake of every predecessor, regardless of the specific leader chosen.

The four-way trap among the United States, Israel, Saudi Arabia, and Iran further compounds the war's dynamics. Each of the three other players ultimately wants to see American power broken in the region. Israel and Saudi Arabia may be official allies of the United States, but their strategic objectives diverge from Washington's stated aims. Israel wants a prolonged conflict that destroys American political will without using nuclear weapons, forcing a ground invasion that exhausts American resources. Saudi Arabia wants Iran destroyed but also wants to humble Israel and, ultimately, the United States. The game theory creates an alignment among three nominal adversaries against a common structural position. This is not a coalition built on shared values. It is a coalition built on shared interest in American overextension.

The combined effect of these structural forces, Professor Jiang argues, points toward a US defeat in any prolonged Iran war. The defeat would not come from a single dramatic battle. It would come from the mathematics of production capacity, the impossibility of installing legitimate governance from outside, the trap of exit creating worse consequences than staying, and the alignment of regional powers who each benefit from American exhaustion. The fuse, as he puts it, is already lit. The question is how long the wick turns out to be.
