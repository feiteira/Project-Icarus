== Professor Jiang's Analysis of Iran ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of Iran draws from eight videos across his predictive history, strategy, and Civilization series. His central prediction, which earned him half a million subscribers when it went viral before the Iran-Israel conflict of April 2026, was that the assassination of General Soleimani in January 2020 had made war between the United States and Iran inevitable, because historically in that region killing an ambassador provokes a war that the political structure cannot avoid. That prediction has now been vindicated, and Professor Jiang's framework for understanding why it happened and what will follow provides the most comprehensive analytical account available in his published work.

The core of Professor Jiang's strategic analysis is the law of asymmetric warfare, which states that whichever side is able to create energy, openness, and cohesion will prevail. The American strategy of decapitation, economic sabotage, and sectarian provocation, which worked in Libya and Syria, will produce the opposite effect in Iran. Decapitation strikes remove ambitious leaders and create a leaner, more meritocratic command structure. Economic destruction of cities unifies the previously divided urban secular and rural religious populations against the common enemy. Arming minorities activates Persian nationalism, which the theocracy had suppressed in favor of a transethnic religious identity. Each element of the American strategy, paradoxically, strengthens the Iranian position. This is not contingent on Iranian leadership being clever; it follows mechanically from the structural dynamics of asymmetric warfare against a society with the depth and cohesion that Iran possesses.

The Iranian strategy in the conflict centers on the destruction of the global economy rather than the defeat of American military forces. Professor Jiang's analysis identifies the vulnerability that Iran can exploit: Qatar provides twenty percent of the world's liquefied natural gas, the GCC states host the energy infrastructure that powers the global economy, and the Strait of Hormuz remains the chokepoint through which oil flows to Asian markets that cannot function without it. Iran's military objective is not to defeat the American fleet in direct confrontation but to make the economic consequences of the conflict so severe that they exceed what the international system can absorb. This is why Iranian attacks target energy infrastructure rather than military installations, and why the Iranian strategy requires the support of China and Russia, whose structural interests align with Tehran's in preventing American hegemonic control of the region.

The three predictions that Professor Jiang offers as the test of his analytical framework are that the United States will launch a ground invasion, that nuclear weapons will not be used, and that the Al-Aqsa mosque will be destroyed. These three predictions are interdependent in ways that make each of them individually more likely than the baseline probability would suggest. The ground invasion is the only path by which the United States and Israel can achieve their stated objective of destroying Iran's capacity to function as a nation state, because air power alone cannot accomplish that goal. The nuclear question is different: the costs of breaking that taboo are so high that Professor Jiang assigns near-complete confidence to the prediction that tactical nuclear weapons will not be used in this conflict. The Al-Aqsa mosque prediction follows from the combination of Israeli eschatological motivations, the chaos of sustained regional warfare, and the structural impossibility of preserving all sacred sites through a conflict of this intensity. Destroying that mosque would obligate the world's two billion Muslims to respond, but Professor Jiang believes the chain of events that leads to its destruction is already set in motion by forces that individual actors cannot control.

The connection between Iran and the broader Eurasian alignment against American hegemony is central to Professor Jiang's analysis. China has a structural dependency on Middle Eastern energy that makes Chinese neutrality in this conflict impossible below the threshold of direct military confrontation with the United States. China will provide weapons, financing, and diplomatic cover for Iran while maintaining the formal posture of a neutral party. Russia shares this interest and adds military capabilities that Iran lacks. The de facto alliance among Iran, China, and Russia is not a coalition of the willing but a structural alignment produced by shared opposition to American unipolar dominance, and it has the depth and durability to outlast the political cycles that will repeatedly pressure American leadership to find an exit from a conflict that cannot be won cheaply or quickly.
