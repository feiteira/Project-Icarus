== Professor Jiang's Analysis of China ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of China draws from eight videos spanning his Civilization series, interviews, and predictive history framework. His analysis operates on multiple timescales simultaneously, connecting the deep structure of Chinese civilization to the immediate geopolitical predicaments of the present era. The framework he has constructed is not area-specific knowledge but a general theory of civilizational dynamics that applies equally to Western and Eastern contexts, and the Chinese case serves as both a test of that theory and a source of some of its most important insights.

The central question Professor Jiang poses about China is why a civilization that gave the world the compass, paper, printing, and gunpowder, and that was the most advanced civilization on earth for centuries, stopped being creative after the Song dynasty around 1200 AD. He frames this through the theory of open cooperative competition developed across his Civilization series. During the Warring States period, China experienced the same dynamics that drove innovation everywhere: multiple states in contact with each other, trading and intermarrying, yet competing ruthlessly for dominance. This openness and competition produced the Hundred Schools of Thought, including Confucianism, Daoism, and Legalism, alongside Sun Tzu's Art of War. Eventually one state, the Qin, emerged not because it was the strongest or most developed but because it was the hungriest, the most willing to adopt innovation, and the most opportunistic. The Qin was the marginal power in the mountains while the richer states in the valleys grew complacent. This is the law that Professor Jiang applies universally: the empire that looks too big to fail has usually already begun its internal corrosion, while the smaller challenger has found the structural crack.

The modern implication of this analysis is that China today occupies the position of the ascending marginal power, while the United States occupies the position of the overextended empire that mistakes scale for destiny. Professor Jiang's predictive history framework identifies the United States as being in structural decline not because of any single policy error but because the American political economy has undergone a transformation he calls the revolt of the elite. Beginning in the 1980s with the Reagan revolution and the shift toward neoliberalism, the organizing principle of American society moved from the worker to the consumer. This is not merely an economic shift but a civilizational one. When the worker was the central unit, society required unions, collective bargaining, healthcare, public education, and affordable universities to reproduce the workforce. When the consumer became the central unit, those social investments became unnecessary costs to be cut. The result was an explosion of inequality from a twenty-to-one CEO-to-worker pay ratio in the 1970s to a two-hundred-to-three-hundred-to-one ratio today, alongside the hollowing out of the middle class that had been the global model's proof of concept.

On the immediate question of US-China relations, Professor Jiang's most distinctive prediction is the coming grand bargain. His framework holds that hegemonic powers do not fight to the finish. When two great powers push confrontation to its maximum, the economic pain on both sides becomes politically unbearable, creating the conditions for a deal neither side could have sold domestically six months earlier. The maximum pressure phase precedes the grand bargain, and Professor Jiang argues the noise of confrontation is itself a signal that resolution is near. He cites the Plaza Accord of 1985, where the US and Japan restructured global currency arrangements after three years of what appeared irreconcilable trade war, and the Nixon-Mao opening of 1972, where total ideological confrontation dissolved into a secret bargain whose announcement shocked the world but whose negotiation both sides had been conducting for months. The same pattern, he argues, is playing out now. By September 2026, back-channel US-China negotiations will become public knowledge, either through a leak or a joint announcement. The deal will involve US concessions on technology restrictions and Chinese concessions on trade arrangements, structured as mutual face-saving rather than capitulation.

China's strategic position regarding the Middle East conflict flows from its structural dependency on energy imports from the region. Professor Jiang explains that most of Southeast Asia, specifically China, Japan, and South Korea, depends on oil from the Middle East. If Iran closes the Strait of Hormuz, this would function as a nuclear option worse than a bomb because it would paralyze the global economy and force Chinese and Japanese military response. This structural interest means China has no choice but to bolster Iran covertly, sending weapons and financing while maintaining the formal posture of not declaring war. China will not send soldiers and will not make the conflict direct, but it will provide the material support that allows Iran to sustain attrition against American precision munitions. The red line for China is direct military confrontation with the United States, but below that threshold, Professor Jiang expects comprehensive covert support.

The deeper analysis of Chinese civilization Professor Jiang develops centers on the concept of the Middle Kingdom not merely as a geographic description but as a civilizational self-understanding. China historically perceived itself as the center of civilization, the model for all humanity, and this self-image created both strength through cultural coherence and weakness through resistance to learning from outside. The Song dynasty failure was not simply technological or economic but conceptual: the civilization stopped being open precisely when it had achieved sufficient scale to believe it had nothing to learn. The parallel to contemporary American decline is structural rather than cultural: in both cases, the empire mistakes its own success for proof of permanent superiority, when the historical record suggests that success of that magnitude is usually the precursor to reversal. The lesson Professor Jiang draws is that civilizational vitality requires the ongoing condition of open cooperative competition, and that condition is precisely what scale destroys.
