== Professor Jiang's Analysis of the United States ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of the United States draws from six videos across his predictive history, Civilization, and strategy series. His framework treats American decline not as a contingent policy failure but as a structural consequence of civilizational transformation that began in the 1980s with the Reagan revolution. The central claim is that American society underwent a fundamental shift in its organizing principle, moving from a culture organized around the worker to one organized around the consumer, and that this shift has produced a set of structural contradictions that no policy intervention can resolve because the contradictions are built into the civilizational orientation itself.

The worker-to-consumer transition is the key to Professor Jiang's analysis of American decline. When the worker was the central unit of social organization, society required unions, collective bargaining, affordable healthcare, public education, and accessible universities to reproduce the workforce that the system needed. When the consumer became the central unit, those social investments became unnecessary costs to be eliminated. Professor Jiang documents the explosion of inequality that followed: the CEO-to-worker pay ratio that was twenty-to-one in the 1970s reached two-hundred-to-three-hundred-to-one by the present era. This is not simply a description of wealth concentration. It is the structural hollowing out of the middle class that had served as the proof of concept for liberal democracy worldwide. When that middle class collapses, the ideological justification for American global leadership collapses with it, because American hegemony was always predicated on the claim that liberal democracy and free markets produced a broadly prosperous and stable society.

The military dimension of American decline follows from this civilizational analysis. Professor Jiang identifies the fundamental problem as an inverted cost pyramid, where the most expensive assets, aircraft carriers and precision munitions, are the most rapidly consumed while the cheapest unit, infantry soldiers, forms the smallest component of the force structure. In any prolonged conflict against a peer or near-peer adversary, the United States cannot replenish its most critical assets fast enough to sustain pressure. The 1991 Gulf War, in Professor Jiang's framework, was a video game rather than a genuine military contest, and the strategic culture that grew from it is dangerously misleading. The real wars, the ones against adversaries with the capacity to adapt and inflict sustained attrition, have not been fought since World War II. The American public and political class have been conditioned to expect cheap, fast victories achieved through air power and technological supremacy, and no such victory is available against a determined opponent with depth, motivation, and external support.

On the immediate geopolitical question of Venezuela and the broader Western Hemisphere, Professor Jiang's analysis focuses on the Monroe Doctrine corollary that Trump has articulated. The National Security Strategy published by the White House identifies Russia's security arrangements with Cuba and China's thirty ports and trade relationships throughout Latin America as existential threats to American imperial control. Ten percent of America's naval assets have been assembled in the Caribbean to enforce what Professor Jiang calls the mineral doctrine, a strategy aimed at demonstrating American capacity to disrupt the trade relationships that China has built in the region. This is not primarily about Venezuela itself, where the United States remains the largest trade partner, but about signaling to Russia and China that American reach extends everywhere their influence has gone. The escalation with Venezuela is a message to Beijing about what happens to nations that develop relationships with American adversaries.

The immediate future that Professor Jiang predicts for the United States involves a contradiction at its core: domestic decline forces greater external aggression, while external aggression accelerates domestic decline. The grand bargain with China that he predicts will be announced in 2026 represents an attempt to stabilize one relationship while the United States continues to overextend in other regions. The American elite understands that prolonged conflict with both China and Iran simultaneously is structurally unsustainable, which is why the back-channel negotiations with Beijing are already underway. But the political logic of American leadership makes concessions difficult to sustain domestically, and Professor Jiang expects the negotiation to be portrayed as a victory for both sides rather than the capitulation that one side will have secretly accepted. The question is not whether American decline will continue but how fast, and what structural realignments will define the post-hegemonic order.
