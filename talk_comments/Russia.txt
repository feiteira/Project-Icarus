== Professor Jiang's Analysis of Russia ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of Russia draws from eight videos spanning his Civilization series, predictive history framework, and strategic analysis of the current conflict between Russia and Ukraine. His central argument is that the Russian invasion of Ukraine cannot be understood through the simple narratives that dominate Western media, whether those narratives frame the conflict as unprovoked aggression, a necessary response to NATO expansion, or a prelude to European conflict more broadly. Instead, Professor Jiang insists that the deeper drivers are historical, sociological, and philosophical in nature, and that Putin himself has articulated these deeper causes in multiple interviews that Western audiences have largely ignored or dismissed as propaganda.

The framework Professor Jiang applies to Russia is the civilizational analysis he has developed across his Civilization series. Russian civilization, he argues, differs fundamentally from both the Anglo-American and German civilizational types in its relationship to fatalism, divinity, and the human heart. Where Americans and British are utilitarians who believe that practical efficacy is the measure of all things, and where Germans are romantics who believe that the individual will must be subordinated to collective spiritual development, Russians are fatalists who believe that human beings cannot control their own destiny, that God is a redemptive and merciful force, and that the deepest knowledge resides not in science or philosophy but in the human heart. This civilizational orientation produces a fundamentally different strategic culture than the one that dominates Western analysis, one in which the willingness to absorb suffering, to accept losses, and to play the long game is not a weakness but a structural advantage.

The historical pattern Professor Jiang identifies as most relevant to understanding the current conflict is the recurring emergence of Russia as the marginal power that eventually overwhelms more developed and wealthier adversaries. Moscow in 1300 was a vassal state of the Mongol Empire, one of many such states paying tribute and providing troops to the imperial center. The historical record shows that it is never the greatest power that eventually overwhelms everyone but always the marginal power, the one that is hungriest, most willing to adopt innovation, and most opportunistic in exploiting the weaknesses of larger and more complacent opponents. Professor Jiang cites the Qing dynasty as a parallel: also a marginal power that emerged from the frontier to conquer the most sophisticated civilization in the world, not through superior technology or wealth but through superior motivation, unity, and adaptive capacity. Russia in the current conflict is operating from the same structural position.

The immediate conflict with Ukraine is shaped by several factors that Western analysis systematically underestimates. First, Professor Jiang argues that NATO expansion was not the cause of the war but the proximate trigger, one that would have had different effects on a Russia with a different strategic culture. What Putin actually wanted, as he has explained in multiple interviews, was a security arrangement that would prevent the emergence of a unified hostile bloc on Russia's western border. Second, the military dynamics of the conflict reveal a fundamental asymmetry between Russian willingness to absorb losses and Ukrainian dependence on external support that cannot be sustained indefinitely. Third, the Russian strategy has been consistently underestimated by Western analysts who expected a quick collapse of Ukrainian resistance and have consistently failed to predict the depth of Russian commitment to achieving its minimum objectives.

The relationship between Russia and China is central to Professor Jiang's strategic analysis. He identifies the emerging alliance between Russia, China, and Iran as the structural response to American unipolar dominance, and he argues that this alliance is held together not by shared values but by shared interest in preventing American hegemonic control of the Eurasian landmass. China needs Russian energy and Russian military capabilities to sustain its conflict with the United States over Taiwan and the South China Sea. Russia needs Chinese economic support to survive the Western sanctions regime. Iran needs both to sustain its resistance to American regional hegemony. The result is a de facto coalition that has the depth, the motivation, and the geographic position to resist American pressure indefinitely, as long as no single member is forced to absorb defeats that it cannot recover from.

Professor Jiang's prediction for the Russia-Ukraine conflict is that Russia will ultimately achieve its minimum strategic objectives, defined not as the conquest of all Ukraine but as the prevention of Ukrainian membership in NATO and the establishment of a neutral buffer state in territory that Russia currently controls or will control by the end of the conflict. The Western expectation of Russian collapse has consistently failed to account for the depth of Russian strategic commitment and the structural advantages of the Russian position, including interior lines, population depth, and the willingness to absorb the economic costs of prolonged conflict. The question is not whether Russia will prevail but how high the cost will be and how the settlement will shape the European security architecture for the next several decades.
