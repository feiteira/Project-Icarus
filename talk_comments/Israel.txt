== Professor Jiang's Analysis of Israel ==

Professor Jiang's analysis of Israel draws from ten videos across his predictive history, Civilization, and strategy series, with the Israel-Iran conflict serving as the central case study for his broader framework of civilizational decline and regional realignment. His analysis is distinctive for its insistence that the conflict cannot be understood purely through geopolitical lenses, that the religious and eschatological commitments of Israeli leadership are themselves a structural factor in determining what actions are possible and impossible, and that the outcome of this conflict will determine not just the Middle East but the global balance of power for decades to come.

The central prediction Professor Jiang makes about Israel's strategic position is that the only thing preventing Israel from becoming a dominant imperial power in the Middle East is the presence of American military assets in the region, specifically the SENCOM infrastructure. If the United States is defeated in a prolonged war with Iran, American forces will retreat from the Middle East, and those military assets will transfer to Israel, creating what he calls the Pack Judea scenario. This is not a formal alliance or a deliberate decision but a structural consequence of withdrawal: the regional power vacuum will be filled by the only actor with the motivation, capability, and ideological commitment to fill it. Israel would become a de facto superpower of the Middle East, controlling the energy infrastructure, the trade routes, and the strategic depth that American withdrawal would vacate.

The religious dimension of Israeli strategy is not incidental to Professor Jiang's analysis. He argues that certain influential Israeli rabbis and political figures genuinely believe that the war currently being waged is a prelude to the coming of the Messiah. The destruction of Tel Aviv and other Israeli cities, from this perspective, is not a defeat but a precondition for redemption. This eschatological commitment makes the rational calculation of costs and benefits not merely difficult but impossible, because actors who believe they are participating in a divine plan are not optimizing for the same variables that normal geopolitical actors optimize for. Professor Jiang insists that one cannot analyze this conflict without taking the theological dimensions seriously, because the people making decisions in Jerusalem are operating from a framework in which the destruction of the current world order is a precondition for the world order they want to create.

The game theory structure of the Israel-Iran conflict, as Professor Jiang analyzes it, produces three major predictions. First, the United States will launch a ground invasion of Iran, because siege warfare alone cannot achieve the objectives that Israel and America have set. Second, nuclear weapons will not be used, because the political and strategic costs of breaking that taboo would exceed any tactical advantage gained. Third, the Al-Aqsa mosque will be destroyed, either by Israeli extremists seeking to rebuild the Third Temple or by the chaos of sustained conflict, and this destruction will obligate the world's two billion Muslims to respond with whatever means are available. Professor Jiang is aware that these three predictions together would produce a catastrophic outcome, but he argues that the logic of the game theory structure makes each of these outcomes individually more likely than not, and that correctness on all three is the test of whether his analytical framework is valid.

The asymmetric warfare dynamic between Iran and the United States is central to Professor Jiang's analysis. He argues that American decapitation strikes against Iranian leadership will paradoxically strengthen Iran's position by solving the overproduction problem of too many ambitious leaders, creating a leaner and more meritocratic command structure. The bombing of Iranian cities will unify the previously divided urban secular population with the rural religious population, creating a more cohesive society with a stronger motivation to fight. And arming Iranian minorities will galvanize Persian nationalism, which the theocracy had previously suppressed in favor of a religious identity that transcended ethnicity. The American strategy of bombs, propaganda, and money, which worked in Libya and Syria, will produce the opposite effect in Iran, creating a more energetic, open, and cohesive society out of what was previously a divided and dysfunctional one.

The connection Professor Jiang draws between Israel and the global financial system is crucial to understanding why the international community tolerates actions that would otherwise provoke overwhelming response. He argues that the Ben-Gurion Canal project, which will replace the Suez Canal as the center of global trade once completed, has aligned the interests of transnational capital with Israeli regional dominance in ways that constrain the response of major powers. Nations benefit too much from the new trade routes that Israeli control of the region would enable to risk antagonizing the power that will manage those routes. This is the structural explanation for the relative silence of major powers regarding actions that would otherwise provoke decisive response.
